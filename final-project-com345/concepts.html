Terms and Concepts

In order to adequetly idenitfy the barriers women face, two key concepts must be defined.

Hypersexualization: When looking at the broadcast field in general, most of the TV faces that we see have hypersexualized women to have higher beauty standards than men. Starting with the news field, women are being put as the weather girl, wearing tight dresses and heavily made up to be as visibly appealing as possible. When connecting to the sports field, most women, especially in football, are seen on the sideline or by dugouts and looking more made up than the male commentators. The thing with football is more that people would care more about how the male commentators are sounding, and caring more about how the sideline reporter is looking.

Gender Polarization: In news rooms, (not seen as much today, but it still happens), women were being assigned softer news stories or more feature pieces rather than hard news. This was done because women did not have the same credibility as men, or seen as being as strong as men. In gender polarization, some traits for women are that they are softer and more delicate than men, which relates them to not being capable of handling a hard news story.

Androcentrism: As the broadcast industry started out being a male dominated field, the terms also became associated with gender. The term “anchorman” is for the host of a news segment, with today having the gender be dropped and the phrase just being “anchor”. Another example is the name of the camera operator being a “cameraman”.

Misogyny:The sports broadcasting field has a form of misogyny which says that “women don’t know sports.” This has been around for many years and especially more frequent in the four major pro sports leagues (NFL, MLB, NBA, & NHL). This is part of the idea that puts women as sideline reporters rather than in the booth calling a game. Not only is there just sideline reporting, but hosting rather than still being able to call the games as it can be seen as just being able facilitate the conversation, and not necessarily being an “expert," despite their experience.

Social Cognitive Theory: Television and media can give people something to try and copy, which can be harmful when people copy the same behaviors. The conversation can then be focused on the younger generation as girls can see the sideline reporters and that’s what they strive to be as their end goal. However, the real goal should be reaching hiring into an executive producer role, which has more power and pays more than the sideline role. The harm is that this sets a bad precedent for girls who strive to break the gender stereotypes as it becomes more seen as a “pretty face” rather than an expert on the specific sport.
